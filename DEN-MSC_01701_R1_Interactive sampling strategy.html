Hacktivist groups
Anonymous hackers that unite around a shared cause — mostly political, but also cultural, patriotic, and religious — and use their skills to support it. Hacktivism involves the nonviolent use of illegal — or legally ambiguous — digital tools. The most (in)famous hacktivist group is Anonymous, which focuses its attacks on political figures, foreign governments, and religious groups.
Organized crime groups
In the past, cybercrime was thought of as a one-man activity; these days the underground is buzzing with organized crime groups. Criminal groups who engage in organized cybercrime usually make use of a variety of methods and threats — hacking, malware creation and distribution, DDoS attacks, social engineering — to inflict harm on their victims. With such a myriad of threats and methods at their disposal, organized crime groups can cause financial, psychological, and economic harm. The main motivation behind the attacks launched by these groups is financial gain.
Malicious insiders
These threat actors target organizations or systems they already have access to. They are usually individuals who intentionally abuse credentials to perform information theft for personal or financial motives. It is also common for these threat actors to leak inside information to the outside world and, in doing so, negatively impact the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of an organization’s information or information systems. An infamous example of a malicious insider is the case of Jean Patrice Delia, a previous employee of General Electric, who stole 8,000 sensitive files from the company with the goal of starting a new competing company.
Advanced technologies are being used in espionage between nation states. Government-sponsored cyberespionage groups target and gain illicit access to other government networks in order to listen in on conversations as well as steal and damage confidential information.

Money
Many threat actors employ cyberthreats to cause damage and ultimately demand ransom to perform damage control and restore networks and systems. Threat actors such as organized crime groups are a good example of this. In these cases, ransomware is the go-to threat type as criminals can encrypt an organization’s critical files and demand ransom in order to decrypt them. These days cybercriminals demand cryptocurrencies as it is often untraceable.
Valuable data
Valuable or sensitive data, such as bank account details, passwords, telephone numbers, etc., is targeted by most cybercriminals as it serves multiple functions. It can be sold on the black market, it can be used to gain further access into other systems or networks, or it can be leaked, thereby damaging the organization’s reputation.
Reputation or brand
Most cyberattacks have the ability to damage the reputation or brand of an organization as it jeopardizes customer trust as well as its industry perception. Hacktivist groups are most likely to target this core asset as their main focus is not the money they can make out of an attack but rather the harm they can inflict on an organization that does not share their views.
An organization’s intellectual property (IP) — be it patents, trade secrets, or employee know-how — is sometimes more valuable than its physical assets, which is why cybercriminals target this core asset. Once threat actors gain access to an organization’s IP, they can use it for financial gain or even reputational damage. Experts claim that this asset has increased in value in the past two decades and it is imperative for organizations to acknowledge this and put systems in place to protect it.
